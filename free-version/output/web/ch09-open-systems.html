    Chapter 9 Open Quantum Systems (Dimension 6)

    Learning Goals

    • Understand the distinction between closed and open quantum systems.
      🔗
    • Derive the Lindblad master equation for Markovian open systems.
      🔗
    • Apply quantum channels and Kraus operators to describe system evolution.
      🔗
    • Analyze decay, dephasing, and thermalization as open-system processes.
      🔗
    • Connect decoherence and dissipation to experimental quantum systems.
      🔗
    🔗
    The sixth dimension of our framework asks: is the system isolated from its environment? In reality, no quantum system is perfectly isolated. Atoms interact with electromagnetic vacuum fluctuations, qubits in quantum computers couple to thermal noise, and electrons in solids scatter from phonons. When we cannot track the environment’s degrees of freedom, the system’s evolution becomes non-unitary. This chapter develops the mathematical framework for open quantum systems—systems coupled to environments they cannot control.
    🔗
    The central tool is the Lindblad master equation, which generalizes the von Neumann equation to include dissipation and decoherence. We also introduce the language of quantum channels and Kraus operators, which provides a complementary perspective. These tools are essential for understanding quantum decoherence, quantum error correction, and the design of practical quantum devices.
    🔗

    Section 9.1 System-Environment Framework

    An open quantum system consists of:
    🔗
    • The system S (the part we observe and control)
      🔗
    • The environment E (everything else—thermal bath, electromagnetic field, etc.)
      🔗
    The total Hamiltonian is:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \HH_{\text{total}} = \HH_S \otimes \hat{I}_E + \hat{I}_S \otimes \HH_E + \HH_{\text{int}}, \end{equation*}
    where \(\HH_{\text{int}}\) couples system and environment. The total state \(\rho_{SE}\) evolves unitarily, but the reduced system state \(\rho_S = \text{Tr}_E(\rho_{SE})\) generally does not.
    🔗
    The key insight: unitary evolution of the total system produces non-unitary evolution of the subsystem. Information flows from system to environment, causing decoherence and dissipation.
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 9.2 The Lindblad Master Equation

    Under the Markov approximation (environment has no memory), the most general form of the master equation that preserves the properties of the density matrix is:
    🔗

    The Lindblad Master Equation.

    Here:
    🔗
    🔗
    The Lindblad equation preserves all properties of a density matrix: Hermiticity, trace, and positivity. It is the quantum analog of the classical Fokker-Planck equation.
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 9.3 Key Examples of Open-System Dynamics

    Subsection 9.3.1 Amplitude Damping (Spontaneous Emission)

    A two-level atom coupled to the electromagnetic vacuum. The excited state \(\ket{1}\) can decay to the ground state \(\ket{0}\) by emitting a photon. The Lindblad operator is:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \hat{L} = \sqrt{\gamma}\ket{0}\bra{1} = \sqrt{\gamma}\sigma_-, \end{equation*}
    where \(\gamma\) is the spontaneous emission rate. The master equation gives:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \rho_{11}(t) = \rho_{11}(0) e^{-\gamma t}, \quad \rho_{00}(t) = 1 - \rho_{11}(0)e^{-\gamma t}, \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \rho_{01}(t) = \rho_{01}(0) e^{-\gamma t/2}. \end{equation*}
    The population decays exponentially with rate \(\gamma\) (T\(_1\) process). The coherence decays at half this rate. For long times, the system relaxes to the ground state.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 9.3.2 Pure Dephasing

    Environmental noise that shifts energy levels without causing transitions. The Lindblad operator is:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \hat{L} = \sqrt{\gamma_\phi}\sigma_z, \end{equation*}
    This destroys off-diagonal coherence without affecting populations:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \rho_{01}(t) = \rho_{01}(0) e^{-2\gamma_\phi t}. \end{equation*}
    The total decoherence rate is:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{T_2} = \frac{1}{2T_1} + \frac{1}{T_\phi}, \end{equation*}
    where \(T_1 = 1/\gamma\) is the energy relaxation time and \(T_\phi = 1/(2\gamma_\phi)\) is the pure dephasing time. In superconducting qubits, typical values are \(T_1 \sim 100\) μs and \(T_2 \sim 50\) μs.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 9.3.3 Thermalization

    At finite temperature, both absorption and emission occur. With Lindblad operators for emission (\(\hat{L}_1 = \sqrt{\gamma(\bar{n}+1)}\sigma_-\)) and absorption (\(\hat{L}_2 = \sqrt{\gamma\bar{n}}\sigma_+\)), where \(\bar{n} = 1/(e^{\hbar\omega/k_BT} - 1)\text{,}\) the system relaxes to the thermal equilibrium state:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \rho_{\text{eq}} = \frac{1}{2\bar{n}+1}\begin{pmatrix} \bar{n}+1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp \bar{n} \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 9.4 Quantum Channels and Kraus Operators

    An alternative description uses quantum channels (completely positive trace-preserving maps). Any physical evolution of a density matrix can be written as:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \rho \to \mathcal{E}(\rho) = \sum_k \hat{K}_k \rho \hat{K}_k^\dagger, \end{equation*}
    where the Kraus operators satisfy \(\sum_k \hat{K}_k^\dagger \hat{K}_k = \hat{I}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 9.1. The Depolarizing Channel.

    With probability \(p\text{,}\) the qubit state is replaced by the maximally mixed state:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{E}(\rho) = (1-p)\rho + \frac{p}{3}(\sigma_x\rho\sigma_x + \sigma_y\rho\sigma_y + \sigma_z\rho\sigma_z). \end{equation*}
    This models uniform random errors and is central to quantum error correction theory.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 9.5 Quantum Trajectories and Quantum Jumps

    The Lindblad master equation describes the average evolution of an ensemble. Individual realizations follow quantum trajectories. Between jumps, the system evolves under an effective non-Hermitian Hamiltonian:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \HH_{\text{eff}} = \HH_S - \frac{i\hbar}{2}\sum_k \gamma_k \hat{L}_k^\dagger \hat{L}_k. \end{equation*}
    At random times, quantum jumps occur: the system undergoes a sudden transformation \(\ket{\psi} \to \hat{L}_k\ket{\psi}/\|\hat{L}_k\ket{\psi}\|\text{.}\) Averaging over many trajectories recovers the Lindblad equation.
    🔗
    This "unraveling" of the master equation is both conceptually illuminating and computationally useful (Monte Carlo wavefunction method).
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 9.6 Chapter Summary

    • Open systems interact with environments, leading to non-unitary evolution of the system state.
      🔗
    • The Lindblad master equation is the most general Markovian equation preserving density matrix properties.
      🔗
    • Amplitude damping models spontaneous emission; pure dephasing models environmental noise.
      🔗
    • The decoherence rate \(1/T_2 = 1/(2T_1) + 1/T_\phi\) combines relaxation and dephasing.
      🔗
    • Quantum channels and Kraus operators provide an operator-sum representation of physical evolution.
      🔗
    • Quantum trajectories unravel the master equation into individual stochastic histories.
      🔗
    • Open-system theory is essential for understanding and designing quantum technologies.
      🔗
    🔗
