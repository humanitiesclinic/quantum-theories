    Chapter 2 The Simplest Case: Non-Relativistic Spinless Schrödinger Equation

    This chapter establishes the foundation by exploring the simplest corner of our 11-dimensional space: non-relativistic, spinless, single-particle quantum mechanics. This is the domain of the time-dependent Schrödinger equation, which governs the vast majority of practical quantum applications from chemistry to condensed matter physics.
    🔗
    Our strategy is to start with the most basic cases (particle in a box, harmonic oscillator), where exact solutions exist, then move to the realistic three-dimensional hydrogen atom. By mastering these fundamental systems, you’ll develop intuition for quantum mechanics that transfers to more complex problems.
    🔗

    Section 2.1 Establishing the Foundation

    We consider a single spinless particle of mass \(m\) in a potential \(V(\mathbf{r}, t)\text{.}\) The time-dependent Schrödinger equation is:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} i\hbar \frac{\partial \psi(\mathbf{r}, t)}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 \psi(\mathbf{r}, t) + V(\mathbf{r}, t) \psi(\mathbf{r}, t) \end{equation*}
    In abstract operator form:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} i\hbar \frac{d \ket{\psi(t)}}{dt} = \HH \ket{\psi(t)} \end{equation*}
    where \(\HH = \frac{\pp^2}{2m} + V(\hat{\mathbf{r}}, t)\) is the Hamiltonian.
    🔗

    Subsection 2.1.1 The Hilbert Space

    Quantum states live in \(L^2(\mathbb{R}^3)\text{,}\) the space of square-integrable functions:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \Hilbert = \{ \psi(\mathbf{r}) : \int_{\mathbb{R}^3} |\psi(\mathbf{r})|^2 d^3r < \infty \} \end{equation*}
    Key structures:
    🔗
    • Inner product: \(\braket{\phi}{\psi} = \int_{\mathbb{R}^3} \phi^*(\mathbf{r}) \psi(\mathbf{r}) d^3r\)
      🔗
    • Norm: \(\|\psi\| = \sqrt{\braket{\psi}{\psi}}\)
      🔗
    • Probability interpretation: \(|\psi(\mathbf{r}, t)|^2\) is the probability density
      🔗
    • Normalization: \(\braket{\psi}{\psi} = 1\) ensures total probability equals one
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.1.2 Operators and Observables

    Position operator (multiplication by \(\mathbf{r}\)):
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \hat{\mathbf{r}} \psi(\mathbf{r}) = \mathbf{r} \psi(\mathbf{r}) \end{equation*}
    Momentum operator (in position representation):
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \hat{\mathbf{p}} = -i\hbar \nabla \end{equation*}
    These satisfy the canonical commutation relations:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} [\hat{r}_i, \hat{p}_j] = i\hbar \delta_{ij} \end{equation*}
    This fundamental commutation relation is the essence of the Heisenberg uncertainty principle.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 2.2 The Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation

    When \(V\) is time-independent, we can separate variables:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \psi(\mathbf{r}, t) = \phi(\mathbf{r}) e^{-iEt/\hbar} \end{equation*}
    This yields the time-independent Schrödinger equation:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \HH \phi(\mathbf{r}) = E \phi(\mathbf{r}) \end{equation*}
    This is an eigenvalue problem: find energy eigenvalues \(E_n\) and eigenstates \(\phi_n(\mathbf{r})\text{.}\)

    Section 2.3 Solved Examples: One-Dimensional Systems

    Subsection 2.3.1 Particle in an Infinite Square Well

    Example 2.1. Infinite Square Well.
    A particle confined to \(0 < x < L\) with infinite potential walls:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} V(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & 0 < x < L \\ \infty & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Boundary conditions: \(\psi(0) = \psi(L) = 0\)
    🔗
    Inside the well: \(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} = E\psi\)
    🔗
    General solution: \(\psi(x) = A\sin(kx) + B\cos(kx)\)
    🔗
    Applying \(\psi(0) = 0\) gives \(B = 0\text{.}\) Applying \(\psi(L) = 0\) gives:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} kL = n\pi, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots \end{equation*}
    Energy eigenvalues:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E_n = \frac{\hbar^2\pi^2 n^2}{2mL^2} \end{equation*}
    Normalized eigenstates:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \psi_n(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}} \sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \end{equation*}
    Key features:
    🔗
    • Quantized energy levels (discrete spectrum)
      🔗
    • Ground state energy \(E_1 = \frac{\hbar^2\pi^2}{2mL^2} > 0\) (zero-point energy)
      🔗
    • Higher states have more oscillations (nodes)
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.3.2 The Harmonic Oscillator

    Example 2.2. Quadratic Potential.
    Potential: \(V(x) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2\)
    🔗
    Energy eigenvalues:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E_n = \hbar\omega\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right), \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots \end{equation*}
    Eigenstates: Involve Hermite polynomials \(H_n\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \psi_n(x) = \left(\frac{m\omega}{\pi\hbar}\right)^{1/4} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n n!}} H_n\left(\sqrt{\frac{m\omega}{\hbar}}x\right) e^{-m\omega x^2/(2\hbar)} \end{equation*}
    Key features:
    🔗
    • Equally spaced levels (spacing \(\hbar\omega\))
      🔗
    • Ground state is Gaussian: \(\psi_0(x) \propto e^{-m\omega x^2/(2\hbar)}\)
      🔗
    • Minimum uncertainty state: \(\Delta x \Delta p = \hbar/2\)
      🔗
    • Ubiquitous in physics: vibrations, QFT, quantum computing
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 2.4 The Hydrogen Atom: Crown Jewel of Quantum Mechanics

    The hydrogen atom—one electron bound to one proton by Coulomb attraction—was the first realistic system exactly solved by quantum mechanics. Its solution validated the entire theory and explained decades of spectroscopic data.
    🔗

    Subsection 2.4.1 The Three-Dimensional Problem

    Hamiltonian (treating the proton as infinitely heavy):
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \HH = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e}\nabla^2 - \frac{ke^2}{r} \end{equation*}
    where \(k = 1/(4\pi\epsilon_0)\) and \(r = |\mathbf{r}|\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The potential \(V(r) = -ke^2/r\) has spherical symmetry, suggesting spherical coordinates \((r, \theta, \phi)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.4.2 Separation in Spherical Coordinates

    The wavefunction factors as:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \psi_{n\ell m}(r, \theta, \phi) = R_{n\ell}(r) Y_\ell^m(\theta, \phi) \end{equation*}
    where:
    🔗
    • \(R_{n\ell}(r)\) is the radial wavefunction
      🔗
    • \(Y_\ell^m(\theta, \phi)\) are spherical harmonics (determine angular distribution)
      🔗
    Three quantum numbers emerge:
    🔗
    • \(n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\) (principal quantum number, determines energy)
      🔗
    • \(\ell = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1\) (orbital angular momentum)
      🔗
    • \(m = -\ell, -\ell+1, \ldots, \ell\) (angular momentum z-component)
      🔗
    Spectroscopic notation: \(s(\ell=0)\text{,}\) \(p(\ell=1)\text{,}\) \(d(\ell=2)\text{,}\) \(f(\ell=3)\text{,}\) etc.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.4.3 Energy Spectrum

    Hydrogen Atom Energy Levels.
    Energy depends only on \(n\text{,}\) not on \(\ell\) or \(m\) (accidental degeneracy in Coulomb potential).
    🔗
    Degeneracy of level \(n\text{:}\) \(\sum_{\ell=0}^{n-1}(2\ell+1) = n^2\) states
    🔗
    Ground state (\(n=1\)): \(E_1 = -13.6 \text{ eV}\) (Rydberg energy)
    🔗
    Ionization energy: \(13.6 \text{ eV}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    This spectrum perfectly matches experimental observations! The success of quantum mechanics in predicting hydrogen’s spectrum was a triumph that established the theory’s validity.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.4.4 Radial Wavefunctions

    Ground state (1s):
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} R_{10}(r) = 2\left(\frac{1}{a_0}\right)^{3/2} e^{-r/a_0} \end{equation*}
    where \(a_0 = \hbar^2/(m_e ke^2) \approx 0.529 \text{ Å}\) is the Bohr radius.
    🔗
    First excited state (2s and 2p):
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} R_{20}(r) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\frac{1}{a_0}\right)^{3/2}\left(1 - \frac{r}{2a_0}\right)e^{-r/(2a_0)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} R_{21}(r) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{6}}\left(\frac{1}{a_0}\right)^{3/2}\frac{r}{a_0}e^{-r/(2a_0)} \end{equation*}
    The radial probability density \(P(r) = r^2|R_{n\ell}(r)|^2\) gives the probability of finding the electron at distance \(r\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Example 2.3. Hydrogen Ground State Radial Distribution.
    For the 1s state:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P_{10}(r) = r^2|R_{10}(r)|^2 = \frac{4r^2}{a_0^3}e^{-2r/a_0} \end{equation*}
    Most probable radius: \(\frac{dP}{dr} = 0\) gives \(r_{\text{max}} = a_0\)
    🔗
    Average radius: \(\expect{r} = \int_0^\infty r P(r) dr = \frac{3}{2}a_0\)
    🔗
    The electron is most likely found at the Bohr radius!
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 2.5 Expectation Values and Measurements

    For an observable \(\AA\) in state \(\ket{\psi}\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \expect{\AA} = \expval{\psi}{\AA}{\psi} = \int \psi^*(\mathbf{r}) \AA \psi(\mathbf{r}) d^3r \end{equation*}

    Example 2.4. Hydrogen Ground State Expectation Values.

    The ground state wavefunction:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \psi_{100}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi a_0^3}} e^{-r/a_0} \end{equation*}
    By spherical symmetry:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \expect{x} = \expect{y} = \expect{z} = 0 \end{equation*}
    But:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \expect{r^2} = 3a_0^2 \end{equation*}
    Kinetic energy:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \expect{T} = \expect{\psi|-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e}\nabla^2|\psi} = \frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e a_0^2} = 13.6 \text{ eV} \end{equation*}
    Potential energy:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \expect{V} = \expect{\psi|-\frac{ke^2}{r}|\psi} = -\frac{ke^2}{a_0} = -27.2 \text{ eV} \end{equation*}
    Total energy:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E_1 = \expect{T} + \expect{V} = -13.6 \text{ eV} \checkmark \end{equation*}
    Virial theorem: \(\expect{T} = -E_1\) and \(\expect{V} = 2E_1\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 2.6 Time Evolution and Transitions

    Superpositions of energy eigenstates oscillate at beat frequencies. For a superposition:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \ket{\psi(t)} = c_1 e^{-iE_1 t/\hbar}\ket{1s} + c_2 e^{-iE_2 t/\hbar}\ket{2s} \end{equation*}
    probability densities oscillate at frequency:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \omega = \frac{E_2 - E_1}{\hbar} \end{equation*}
    When an atom transitions from an excited state to a lower state, it emits a photon with frequency determined by the energy difference:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \hbar\omega_{\text{photon}} = E_{\text{upper}} - E_{\text{lower}} \end{equation*}
    For hydrogen transitions:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \omega_{n \to n'} = \frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{\hbar}\left(\frac{1}{n'^2} - \frac{1}{n^2}\right) \end{equation*}
    This explains atomic spectral lines: the Lyman series (\(n \to 1\)), Balmer series (\(n \to 2\)), Paschen series (\(n \to 3\)), etc.
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 2.7 Chapter Summary

    • Time-dependent Schrödinger equation: \(i\hbar d\ket{\psi}/dt = \HH\ket{\psi}\)
      🔗
    • For time-independent Hamiltonians, separate variables: \(\psi(\mathbf{r}, t) = \phi(\mathbf{r})e^{-iEt/\hbar}\)
      🔗
    • Time-independent equation \(\HH\phi = E\phi\) is an eigenvalue problem
      🔗
    • Infinite square well: quantized energies \(E_n \propto n^2\text{,}\) particle-in-a-box model
      🔗
    • Harmonic oscillator: equally spaced levels \(E_n = \hbar\omega(n + 1/2)\text{,}\) ubiquitous in physics
      🔗
    • Hydrogen atom: three quantum numbers \((n, \ell, m)\text{,}\) energy \(E_n = -13.6 \text{ eV}/n^2\)
      🔗
    • Spherical harmonics determine angular distribution; radial functions describe distance from nucleus
      🔗
    • Expectation values \(\expect{\AA} = \expval{\psi}{\AA}{\psi}\) predict measurement outcomes
