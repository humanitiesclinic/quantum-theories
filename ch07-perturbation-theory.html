    Chapter 7 Approximation Methods: Perturbation Theory (Dimension 9)

    Learning Goals

    • Master time-independent perturbation theory for non-degenerate and degenerate systems.
      🔗
    • Develop time-dependent perturbation theory and transition rates.
      🔗
    • Derive Fermi’s golden rule for transition probabilities.
      🔗
    • Apply the variational method to estimate ground-state energies.
      🔗
    • Understand the sudden and adiabatic approximations and the Berry phase.
      🔗
    • Recognize when each approximation method is appropriate within the 11-dimensional framework.
      🔗
    🔗
    The ninth dimension of our framework concerns the level of approximation used to solve quantum problems. Exact analytical solutions exist only for a handful of systems—the harmonic oscillator, hydrogen atom, and a few others. For virtually every other system, we need approximation methods. This chapter develops the most powerful and widely used techniques: perturbation theory (both time-independent and time-dependent), the variational method, and the sudden and adiabatic approximations.
    🔗
    These methods are not mere computational tricks. They reveal physical structure. Perturbation theory shows how a system responds to small perturbations and predicts atomic spectra with extraordinary precision. The variational principle provides rigorous bounds on energies and underlies computational chemistry. The adiabatic theorem introduces the Berry phase, connecting quantum mechanics to topology. Together, these methods form the practical backbone of quantum mechanics.
    🔗

    Section 7.1 Time-Independent Perturbation Theory: Non-Degenerate Case

    Consider a Hamiltonian split into a solvable part and a small perturbation:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \HH = \HH_0 + \lambda \hat{V}, \end{equation*}
    where \(\lambda\) is a small dimensionless parameter and the eigenstates and eigenvalues of \(\HH_0\) are known:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \HH_0 \ket{n^{(0)}} = E_n^{(0)} \ket{n^{(0)}}. \end{equation*}
    We expand the exact eigenstate and energy in powers of \(\lambda\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \ket{n} = \ket{n^{(0)}} + \lambda \ket{n^{(1)}} + \lambda^2 \ket{n^{(2)}} + \cdots, \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} E_n = E_n^{(0)} + \lambda E_n^{(1)} + \lambda^2 E_n^{(2)} + \cdots. \end{equation*}

    Perturbation Theory Formulas (Non-Degenerate).

    First-order energy correction:
    🔗
    The first-order energy shift is the expectation value of the perturbation in the unperturbed state.
    🔗
    First-order state correction:

    where \(V_{mn} = \bra{m^{(0)}} \hat{V} \ket{n^{(0)}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Second-order energy correction:
    🔗
    The second-order correction always lowers the ground-state energy (all denominators are negative for \(m \neq 0\)).
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 7.1. Anharmonic Oscillator.

    Consider a harmonic oscillator with a quartic perturbation \(V = \lambda x^4\text{.}\) Using ladder operators, \(x = \sqrt{\hbar/(2m\omega)}(\hat{a} + \hat{a}^\dagger)\text{,}\) we compute the first-order correction:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E_n^{(1)} = \lambda \bra{n} x^4 \ket{n} = \frac{3\lambda\hbar^2}{4m^2\omega^2}(2n^2 + 2n + 1). \end{equation*}
    This gives energy levels that are no longer equally spaced—the hallmark of anharmonicity. The ground state shifts by \(E_0^{(1)} = 3\lambda\hbar^2/(4m^2\omega^2)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 7.2. Validity of Perturbation Theory.

    Perturbation theory is valid when \(|V_{mn}| \ll |E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}|\) for all \(m \neq n\text{.}\) When nearby levels are close in energy, the denominators become small and the expansion breaks down. This signals the need for degenerate perturbation theory.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 7.2 Degenerate Perturbation Theory

    When the unperturbed level \(E_n^{(0)}\) is degenerate—multiple states share the same energy—the standard formulas fail because denominators vanish. The resolution is to diagonalize the perturbation within the degenerate subspace.
    🔗
    Suppose states \(\ket{n, \alpha}\) for \(\alpha = 1, 2, \ldots, g\) are degenerate with energy \(E_n^{(0)}\text{.}\) Form the \(g \times g\) matrix:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} W_{\alpha\beta} = \bra{n, \alpha} \hat{V} \ket{n, \beta}. \end{equation*}
    The first-order energy corrections are the eigenvalues of \(W\text{.}\) The correct zeroth-order states are the eigenvectors of \(W\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 7.3. Linear Stark Effect in Hydrogen.

    An external electric field \(\mathcal{E}\) along the z-axis gives perturbation \(\hat{V} = e\mathcal{E}z\text{.}\) For the \(n=2\) level of hydrogen (four-fold degenerate: 2s, 2p\(_0\text{,}\) 2p\(_{\pm 1}\)):
    🔗
    The only non-zero matrix element within this subspace is between 2s and 2p\(_0\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \bra{200} e\mathcal{E}z \ket{210} = -3e\mathcal{E}a_0. \end{equation*}
    Diagonalizing the \(2 \times 2\) matrix in the 2s–2p\(_0\) subspace gives first-order corrections:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E^{(1)} = \pm 3e\mathcal{E}a_0. \end{equation*}
    The energy splitting is linear in \(\mathcal{E}\)—the linear Stark effect. States 2p\(_{\pm 1}\) are unaffected to first order.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 7.4. Zeeman Effect.

    A magnetic field \(\mathbf{B} = B\hat{z}\) perturbs the hydrogen atom via:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \hat{V} = -\hat{\mu} \cdot \mathbf{B} = \frac{eB}{2m_e c}(\hat{L}_z + 2\hat{S}_z). \end{equation*}
    For the anomalous Zeeman effect (including spin), the first-order energy shift for state \(\ket{n, \ell, j, m_j}\) is:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E^{(1)} = \mu_B B m_j g_j, \end{equation*}
    where \(\mu_B = e\hbar/(2m_e c)\) is the Bohr magneton and \(g_j\) is the Landé g-factor:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} g_j = 1 + \frac{j(j+1) + s(s+1) - \ell(\ell+1)}{2j(j+1)}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 7.3 Time-Dependent Perturbation Theory

    When a perturbation is turned on at some time, we use time-dependent perturbation theory. The Hamiltonian is \(\HH(t) = \HH_0 + V(t)\) where \(V(t) = 0\) for \(t \lt 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Working in the interaction picture, the first-order transition amplitude from state \(\ket{i}\) to state \(\ket{f}\) is:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} c_f^{(1)}(t) = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t dt' \, V_{fi}(t') \, e^{i\omega_{fi}t'}, \end{equation*}
    where \(\omega_{fi} = (E_f - E_i)/\hbar\) and \(V_{fi}(t) = \bra{f^{(0)}} V(t) \ket{i^{(0)}}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Subsection 7.3.1 Harmonic Perturbation

    For a sinusoidal perturbation \(V(t) = V_0 \cos(\omega t)\text{,}\) the transition probability is strongly peaked near resonance \(\omega \approx \omega_{fi}\text{.}\) Near resonance, only the co-rotating term matters:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P_{i \to f}(t) \approx \frac{|V_{fi}|^2}{\hbar^2} \frac{\sin^2[(\omega_{fi} - \omega)t/2]}{[(\omega_{fi} - \omega)/2]^2}. \end{equation*}
    This shows that transitions are most probable when the driving frequency matches the transition frequency—the resonance condition \(\omega = \omega_{fi}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 7.3.2 Fermi’s Golden Rule

    Fermi’s Golden Rule.
    For transitions to a continuum of final states with density of states \(\rho(E_f)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    Applications include:
    🔗
    🔗
    The rate is proportional to the square of the matrix element (coupling strength) times the density of states (available final states). Both must be favorable for fast transitions.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 7.4 The Variational Method

    The variational method provides rigorous upper bounds on the ground-state energy without solving the Schrödinger equation exactly.
    🔗

    The Variational Principle.

    For any normalized trial state \(\ket{\psi_{\text{trial}}}\text{:}\)
    🔗
    The true ground-state energy \(E_0\) is always less than or equal to the energy expectation value in any trial state. Equality holds only when \(\ket{\psi_{\text{trial}}}\) is the exact ground state.
    🔗
    🔗
    The strategy: choose a trial wavefunction with adjustable parameters, compute the energy expectation value, and minimize with respect to all parameters.
    🔗

    Example 7.5. Helium Ground State by the Variational Method.

    Using a hydrogen-like trial function with effective charge \(Z_{\text{eff}}\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \psi(\mathbf{r}_1, \mathbf{r}_2) = \frac{Z_{\text{eff}}^3}{\pi a_0^3} e^{-Z_{\text{eff}}(r_1 + r_2)/a_0}. \end{equation*}
    The energy as a function of \(Z_{\text{eff}}\) is:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E(Z_{\text{eff}}) = \left(Z_{\text{eff}}^2 - \frac{27}{8}Z_{\text{eff}}\right) E_1, \end{equation*}
    where \(E_1 = -13.6\) eV. Minimizing: \(Z_{\text{eff}} = 27/16 \approx 1.69\text{,}\) giving \(E \approx -77.5\) eV (experiment: \(-79.0\) eV, 2% error). The effective charge \(Z_{\text{eff}} \lt 2\) reflects electron screening.
    🔗
    🔗
    The Ritz variational method extends this by expanding in a basis: \(\ket{\psi_{\text{trial}}} = \sum_k c_k \ket{\phi_k}\text{.}\) Minimizing energy with respect to the coefficients \(c_k\) leads to a generalized eigenvalue problem. This is the foundation of computational quantum chemistry (Hartree-Fock method, configuration interaction).
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 7.5 Sudden and Adiabatic Approximations

    Subsection 7.5.1 The Sudden Approximation

    When a perturbation is applied very quickly (faster than any internal timescale), the state has no time to adjust. The system remains in its original state, but this state is no longer an eigenstate of the new Hamiltonian. The transition probability to new eigenstate \(\ket{n'}\) is:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P_{n \to n'} = |\braket{n'}{n}|^2, \end{equation*}
    simply the overlap between old and new eigenstates.
    🔗
    Example 7.6. Sudden Expansion of a Box.
    A particle in the ground state of a box of width \(L\text{.}\) The wall instantly moves to \(2L\text{.}\) The probability of remaining in the ground state of the expanded box is:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P = \left|\int_0^L \sqrt{\frac{2}{2L}} \sin\left(\frac{\pi x}{2L}\right) \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}} \sin\left(\frac{\pi x}{L}\right) dx\right|^2 = \left(\frac{8}{3\pi}\right)^2 \approx 0.72. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    🔗

    The Berry phase depends only on the path traced in parameter space, not on the speed:

    Section 7.6 Approximation Methods in the 11-Dimensional Framework

    The ninth dimension classifies the level of approximation:
    🔗
    • Exact: harmonic oscillator, hydrogen atom, two-level systems—rare but foundational.
      🔗
    • Perturbative: fine structure, Stark/Zeeman effects, QED corrections—the workhorse method.
      🔗
    • Variational: helium, molecular bonding, many-body ground states—rigorous bounds.
      🔗
    • Semiclassical: WKB tunneling, coherent states—bridges quantum and classical (Chapter 14).
      🔗
    The art of quantum mechanics is choosing the right approximation for the problem at hand. Perturbation theory requires a natural small parameter. The variational method works when you need ground-state properties. Semiclassical methods apply when \(\hbar\) is effectively small. Mastering approximation means knowing which tool to deploy and when.
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 7.7 Chapter Summary

    • Time-independent perturbation theory expands energies and states in powers of the perturbation.
      🔗
    • First-order energy: \(E_n^{(1)} = \bra{n^{(0)}} \hat{V} \ket{n^{(0)}}\text{;}\) second-order: \(E_n^{(2)} = \sum_{m \neq n} |V_{mn}|^2/(E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)})\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Degenerate perturbation theory requires diagonalizing \(\hat{V}\) within the degenerate subspace.
      🔗
    • Key applications: fine structure, Stark effect, Zeeman effect, anharmonic oscillators.
      🔗
    • Time-dependent perturbation theory gives transition amplitudes driven by external fields.
      🔗
    • Fermi’s golden rule: \(\Gamma = (2\pi/\hbar)|V_{fi}|^2 \rho(E_f)\) for continuum transitions.
      🔗
    • The variational principle: \(E_0 \leq \expect{\HH}_{\text{trial}}\) provides rigorous upper bounds.
      🔗
    • The sudden approximation applies for fast changes; the adiabatic theorem for slow changes.
      🔗
    • The Berry phase is a geometric phase acquired during adiabatic transport, with deep connections to topology.
      🔗
    🔗
