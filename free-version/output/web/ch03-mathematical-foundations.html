    Chapter 3 Mathematical Foundations of Quantum Mechanics

    Before exploring variations in our 11-dimensional framework, we must establish the mathematical machinery that underlies all quantum theory. This chapter develops essential tools: Hilbert spaces, operators, the five fundamental postulates, measurement theory, and uncertainty relations.
    🔗
    Unlike classical mechanics, where states are points in phase space, quantum states are vectors in an infinite-dimensional complex vector space. This mathematical structure, combined with the probabilistic interpretation, gives quantum mechanics its distinctive character.
    🔗

    Section 3.1 Hilbert Spaces

    Subsection 3.1.1 Definition and Structure

    A Hilbert space \(\Hilbert\) is a complete inner product space over the complex numbers. For quantum mechanics:
    🔗
    Essential Properties of a Hilbert Space.
    1. Vector space structure: Linear combinations of states are states:
    🔗
    2. Inner product: \(\braket{\phi}{\psi} \in \mathbb{C}\) satisfying:
    🔗
    3. Completeness: Every Cauchy sequence converges
    🔗
    4. Separability: Countable dense subset exists
    🔗
    🔗
    The norm induced by the inner product is \(\|\psi\| = \sqrt{\braket{\psi}{\psi}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.2 Examples of Hilbert Spaces

    Finite-dimensional: \(\mathbb{C}^n\) with standard inner product: \(\braket{\phi}{\psi} = \sum_{i=1}^n \phi_i^* \psi_i\text{.}\) Used for spin systems, qubits, finite-level atoms.
    🔗
    \(L^2(\mathbb{R}^3)\text{:}\) Square-integrable functions
    \begin{equation*} \Hilbert = \{\psi: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{C} : \int_{\mathbb{R}^3} |\psi(\mathbf{r})|^2 d^3r < \infty\} \end{equation*}
    Inner product: \(\braket{\phi}{\psi} = \int_{\mathbb{R}^3} \phi^*(\mathbf{r}) \psi(\mathbf{r}) d^3r\)
    🔗
    Primary Hilbert space for single-particle quantum mechanics.
    🔗
    Fock space: Direct sum for variable particle number
    \begin{equation*} \Fock = \mathbb{C} \oplus \Hilbert_1 \oplus \Hilbert_2 \oplus \cdots \end{equation*}
    Essential for quantum field theory.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.3 Dirac Notation

    Paul Dirac’s bra-ket notation elegantly separates vectors from functionals:
    🔗
    • Ket: \(\ket{\psi} \in \Hilbert\) is a state vector
      🔗
    • Bra: \(\bra{\psi} \in \Hilbert^*\) is the dual (a linear functional)
      🔗
    • Inner product: \(\braket{\phi}{\psi} \in \mathbb{C}\) is the action of \(\bra{\phi}\) on \(\ket{\psi}\)
      🔗
    • Outer product: \(\ketbra{\psi}{\phi}\) is an operator on \(\Hilbert\)
      🔗
    The bra corresponding to \(\ket{\psi}\) is defined by the adjoint relationship: \(\braket{\phi}{\psi} = \braket{\psi}{\phi}^*\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 3.2 Operators on Hilbert Space

    Subsection 3.2.1 Linear Operators

    An operator \(\AA: \Hilbert \to \Hilbert\) is linear if:
    \begin{equation*} \AA(\alpha\ket{\psi} + \beta\ket{\phi}) = \alpha \AA\ket{\psi} + \beta \AA\ket{\phi} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    In Dirac notation, matrix elements are:
    \begin{equation*} A_{ij} = \braket{\phi_i}{\AA}{\phi_j} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Example: Position operator \(\hat{x}\) in position representation:
    \begin{equation*} \hat{x} \psi(x) = x \psi(x) \end{equation*}
    Acts by multiplication.
    🔗
    Example: Momentum operator \(\hat{p} = -i\hbar d/dx\) in position representation.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.2.2 Hermitian (Self-Adjoint) Operators

    The adjoint \(\AA^\dagger\) of operator \(\AA\) satisfies:
    \begin{equation*} \braket{\phi}{\AA}{\psi} = \braket{\AA^\dagger \phi}{\psi} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    An operator is Hermitian if \(\AA^\dagger = \AA\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Fundamental Theorem for Hermitian Operators.
    Hermitian operators have:
    🔗
    These properties make Hermitian operators suitable for representing physical observables.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.2.3 Unitary Operators

    An operator \(\UU\) is unitary if:
    \begin{equation*} \UU^\dagger \UU = \UU \UU^\dagger = \II \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Unitary operators:
    🔗
    • Preserve inner products: \(\braket{\UU\phi}{\UU\psi} = \braket{\phi}{\psi}\)
      🔗
    • Preserve norms: \(\|\UU\psi\| = \|\psi\|\)
      🔗
    • Represent symmetry transformations and time evolution
      🔗
    The time evolution operator \(U(t) = e^{-i\HH t/\hbar}\) is unitary because \(\HH\) is Hermitian.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 3.3 Commutators and Uncertainty Relations

    Subsection 3.3.1 The Commutator

    The commutator of two operators:
    \begin{equation*} [\AA, \BB] = \AA\BB - \BB\AA \end{equation*}
    🔗
    If \([\AA, \BB] = 0\text{,}\) the operators commute and can be simultaneously diagonalized.
    🔗
    Key examples:
    🔗
    • Canonical: \([\hat{r}_i, \hat{p}_j] = i\hbar\delta_{ij}\)
      🔗
    • Angular momentum: \([\hat{L}_i, \hat{L}_j] = i\hbar\epsilon_{ijk}\hat{L}_k\)
      🔗
    • Harmonic oscillator: \([\hat{a}, \hat{a}^\dagger] = 1\)
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.3.2 Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

    For any two observables \(\AA\) and \(\BB\text{:}\)
    🔗
    Generalized Uncertainty Relation.
    where \(\Delta A = \sqrt{\expect{\AA^2} - \expect{\AA}^2}\) is the standard deviation.
    🔗
    🔗
    For position and momentum:
    \begin{equation*} \Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This is not a statement about measurement disturbance, but a fundamental property of quantum states. The Gaussian wavepacket (harmonic oscillator ground state) achieves the minimum uncertainty.
    🔗

    Section 3.4 The Five Postulates of Quantum Mechanics

    Fundamental Axioms of Quantum Mechanics.

    Postulate 1 (States): The state of a quantum system is represented by a unit vector \(\ket{\psi}\) in a Hilbert space \(\Hilbert\text{.}\) Physically equivalent states differ only by a global phase factor \(e^{i\theta}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Postulate 2 (Observables): Physical observables correspond to Hermitian operators on \(\Hilbert\text{.}\) The possible measurement outcomes are the eigenvalues of the operator.
    🔗
    Postulate 3 (Measurement): When measuring observable \(\AA\) on state \(\ket{\psi}\text{:}\)
    • The probability of obtaining eigenvalue \(a_n\) is \(|c_n|^2 = |\braket{a_n}{\psi}|^2\)
      🔗
    • After measurement yielding \(a_n\text{,}\) the state collapses to \(\ket{a_n}\)
      🔗
    🔗
    Postulate 4 (Time Evolution): The time evolution of a closed system is governed by the Schrödinger equation:
    \begin{equation*} i\hbar \frac{d\ket{\psi}}{dt} = \HH\ket{\psi} \end{equation*}
    where \(\HH\) is the Hamiltonian operator.
    🔗
    Postulate 5 (Composite Systems): The Hilbert space of a composite system is the tensor product of the component Hilbert spaces:
    \begin{equation*} \Hilbert_{\text{total}} = \Hilbert_A \otimes \Hilbert_B \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    These postulates, though concise, contain the entire structure of quantum mechanics. Everything else—interference, entanglement, uncertainty—follows from them.
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 3.5 Measurement Theory

    Subsection 3.5.1 The Born Rule

    Given a normalized state \(\ket{\psi} = \sum_n c_n \ket{\phi_n}\) where \(\ket{\phi_n}\) are eigenstates of observable \(\AA\) with eigenvalues \(a_n\text{:}\)
    🔗
    Probability of measuring \(a_n\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} P(a_n) = |c_n|^2 = |\braket{\phi_n}{\psi}|^2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Expectation value:
    \begin{equation*} \expect{\AA} = \sum_n a_n P(a_n) = \sum_n a_n |\braket{\phi_n}{\psi}|^2 = \braket{\psi}{\AA}{\psi} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The Born rule is fundamental to quantum probability.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.5.2 Measurement and Collapse

    The measurement process has two aspects:
    🔗
    1. Probabilistic outcome: Measurement yields eigenvalue \(a_n\) with probability \(|\braket{a_n}{\psi}|^2\)
      🔗
    2. State reduction: After measurement, the state becomes:
      \begin{equation*} \ket{\psi} \to \ket{\psi'} = \frac{\ketbra{a_n}{a_n}}{\braket{a_n}{\psi}}\ket{\psi} = \ket{a_n} \end{equation*}
      This "collapse" is instantaneous and non-unitary, unlike Schrödinger evolution.
      🔗
    This raises the measurement problem: how to interpret the collapse? Various interpretations exist (Copenhagen, many-worlds, pilot wave), but all give identical predictions for experimental outcomes.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.5.3 Compatible and Incompatible Observables

    Two observables \(\AA\) and \(\BB\) are compatible if \([\AA, \BB] = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Compatible observables:
    🔗
    • Can be simultaneously measured
      🔗
    • Share a common eigenbasis
      🔗
    • No uncertainty limit: \(\Delta A \cdot \Delta B = 0\) is possible
      🔗
    Incompatible observables (\([\AA, \BB] \neq 0\)):
    🔗
    • Cannot be simultaneously measured with arbitrary precision
      🔗
    • No common eigenbasis
      🔗
    • Fundamental uncertainty: \(\Delta A \cdot \Delta B \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}|\expect{[\AA, \BB]}|\)
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 3.6 Basis Representations

    Subsection 3.6.1 Position Representation

    Eigenstates of position: \(\hat{\mathbf{r}}\ket{\mathbf{r}} = \mathbf{r}\ket{\mathbf{r}}\)
    🔗
    Completeness: \(\int d^3r \ketbra{\mathbf{r}}{\mathbf{r}} = \II\)
    🔗
    Any state expands as:
    \begin{equation*} \ket{\psi} = \int d^3r \ket{\mathbf{r}}\braket{\mathbf{r}}{\psi} = \int d^3r \psi(\mathbf{r})\ket{\mathbf{r}} \end{equation*}
    where \(\psi(\mathbf{r}) = \braket{\mathbf{r}}{\psi}\) is the position-space wavefunction.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.6.2 Momentum Representation

    Eigenstates of momentum: \(\hat{\mathbf{p}}\ket{\mathbf{p}} = \mathbf{p}\ket{\mathbf{p}}\)
    🔗
    Completeness: \(\int d^3p \ketbra{\mathbf{p}}{\mathbf{p}} = \II\)
    🔗
    Momentum-space wavefunction:
    \begin{equation*} \tilde{\psi}(\mathbf{p}) = \braket{\mathbf{p}}{\psi} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Connected to position space by Fourier transform.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 3.7 Chapter Summary

    • Quantum states are vectors in a complex Hilbert space with inner product structure
      🔗
    • Key examples: \(\mathbb{C}^n\) (finite), \(L^2(\mathbb{R}^3)\) (single particle), Fock space (fields)
      🔗
    • Dirac notation elegantly separates kets (vectors) from bras (dual functionals)
      🔗
    • Physical observables are Hermitian operators with real eigenvalues
      🔗
    • Unitary operators preserve inner products and represent symmetries/time evolution
      🔗
    • Commutator \([\AA, \BB]\) measures non-commutativity, leads to uncertainty relations
      🔗
    • Five postulates encode complete structure of quantum mechanics
      🔗
    • Born rule: Probability \(P(a_n) = |\braket{a_n}{\psi}|^2\text{,}\) expectation \(\expect{\AA} = \braket{\psi}{\AA}{\psi}\)
      🔗
    • Measurement causes state collapse (reduction) to eigenstate
      🔗
    • Position and momentum representations connected by Fourier transform
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
