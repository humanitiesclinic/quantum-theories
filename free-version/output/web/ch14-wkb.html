    Chapter 14 WKB and Semiclassical Methods (Dimension 10)

    Learning Goals

    • Derive the WKB approximation for the Schrödinger equation.
      🔗
    • Apply connection formulas at classical turning points.
      🔗
    • Calculate tunneling rates through potential barriers.
      🔗
    • Understand the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization condition.
      🔗
    • Connect quantum mechanics to classical mechanics through the Ehrenfest theorem and coherent states.
      🔗
    🔗
    The tenth dimension of our framework concerns the classical limit: how does quantum mechanics reduce to classical mechanics? Semiclassical methods provide the bridge. The WKB (Wentzel-Kramers-Brillouin) approximation is a systematic expansion in powers of \(\hbar\) that captures the leading quantum corrections to classical motion. It is especially powerful for tunneling problems, where classically forbidden processes occur with exponentially small but finite probability.
    🔗
    Semiclassical methods also illuminate the conceptual relationship between quantum and classical mechanics. The Ehrenfest theorem shows that expectation values follow classical trajectories. Coherent states minimize uncertainty and evolve classically. These connections are not merely pedagogical—they are essential for understanding quantum optics, molecular dynamics, and the emergence of classicality.
    🔗

    Section 14.2 Connection Formulas at Turning Points

    Near a turning point \(x = a\) where \(V(a) = E\text{,}\) the WKB solutions must be matched using Airy functions. The connection formulas relate the oscillatory solution on the allowed side to the exponential solution on the forbidden side:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\sqrt{p}}\cos\left(\frac{1}{\hbar}\int_a^x p\,dx' - \frac{\pi}{4}\right) \leftrightarrow \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\kappa}}\exp\left(-\frac{1}{\hbar}\int_x^a \kappa\,dx'\right). \end{equation*}
    These connection formulas are essential for correct quantization conditions and tunneling calculations.
    🔗
    🔗

    🔗

    Example 14.1. Harmonic Oscillator via WKB.

    For \(V = m\omega^2x^2/2\text{,}\) the turning points are \(x = \pm\sqrt{2E/(m\omega^2)}\text{.}\) The Bohr-Sommerfeld integral gives:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_{-a}^{a} \sqrt{2m(E - m\omega^2x^2/2)}\,dx = \frac{\pi E}{\omega} = (n+1/2)\pi\hbar, \end{equation*}
    yielding \(E_n = \hbar\omega(n + 1/2)\)—the exact result.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 14.4 Quantum Tunneling

    A particle encountering a potential barrier \(V(x) \gt E\) in the region \(a \lt x \lt b\) has a transmission coefficient:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} T \approx \exp\left(-\frac{2}{\hbar}\int_a^b \sqrt{2m(V(x)-E)}\,dx\right). \end{equation*}
    The exponential dependence on the barrier parameters makes tunneling extremely sensitive to barrier height and width.
    🔗

    Example 14.2. Alpha Decay.

    An alpha particle (mass \(m_\alpha\text{,}\) energy \(E\)) tunnels through the Coulomb barrier of a nucleus (charge \(Z\)). The Gamow factor gives the tunneling probability:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} T \propto e^{-2\pi Z\alpha c/(v)}, \end{equation*}
    where \(v\) is the alpha particle velocity and \(\alpha\) is the fine-structure constant. This explains the enormous range of alpha-decay lifetimes (from microseconds to billions of years) through modest changes in nuclear parameters.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 14.5 Ehrenfest’s Theorem and Coherent States

    Ehrenfest’s theorem shows that quantum expectation values satisfy classical equations of motion:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m\frac{d^2\expect{x}}{dt^2} = -\expect{\frac{dV}{dx}}. \end{equation*}
    This equals Newton’s second law when \(\expect{dV/dx} \approx dV(\expect{x})/dx\text{,}\) which holds for narrow wavepackets in smooth potentials.
    🔗
    Coherent states \(\ket{\alpha}\) of the harmonic oscillator are the quantum states that most closely mimic classical oscillatory motion:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \ket{\alpha} = e^{-|\alpha|^2/2}\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\alpha^n}{\sqrt{n!}}\ket{n}. \end{equation*}
    They maintain minimum uncertainty \(\Delta x\Delta p = \hbar/2\) at all times and oscillate without spreading—the most "classical" quantum states.
    🔗
    🔗

    Section 14.6 Chapter Summary

    • The WKB approximation is a semiclassical expansion valid when \(\hbar\) is effectively small.
      🔗
    • WKB wavefunctions: \(\psi \sim p^{-1/2} e^{\pm i\int p\,dx/\hbar}\) in allowed regions, exponential in forbidden regions.
      🔗
    • Connection formulas link solutions across classical turning points.
      🔗
    • Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization: \(\oint p\,dx = (n+1/2)\pi\hbar\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Tunneling probability: \(T \sim e^{-2\int \kappa\,dx/\hbar}\text{,}\) exponentially sensitive to barrier parameters.
      🔗
    • Ehrenfest’s theorem connects quantum expectation values to classical equations of motion.
      🔗
    • Coherent states are minimum-uncertainty states that evolve classically.
      🔗
